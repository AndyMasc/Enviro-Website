What is Climate Change

The Truths. The Myths. And Everything in Between.

Climate Change

"A change in global or regional climate patterns, in particular a change apparent from the mid to late 20th century onwards and attributed largely to the increased levels of atmospheric carbon dioxide produced by the use of fossil fuels."

- Oxford Dictionary -

Truths vs Myths

Climate change is happening:

Global surface temperatures have risen by about 1.2°C since the late 19th century, as verified by NASA, NOAA, and the IPCC. Glaciers are retreating, sea levels are rising, and extreme weather events are increasing.

Climate change is natural:

While Earth’s climate has always fluctuated, the current rate and scale of warming cannot be explained by natural factors alone (e.g., solar activity, volcanic eruptions). Models that exclude human greenhouse gas emissions fail to reproduce observed warming trends.

Carbon dioxide is the main driver of recent warming:

CO₂ concentrations are now over 420 ppm, the highest in at least 800,000 years. Its radiative forcing (the heat-trapping effect) is well understood in physics.

CO₂ is a tiny part of the atmosphere, so it can’t make a difference:

Even trace gases can have powerful effects—just as a small concentration of ozone blocks harmful UV radiation. CO₂’s role in trapping infrared radiation is well-documented since the 19th century.

The Doomsday Narrative

The "doomsday narrative" for climate change portrays catastrophic outcomes like civilization collapse and irreversible environmental destruction, sometimes fueled by extreme emissions scenarios or the concept of "tipping points". Critics argue this narrative can be misleading and socially destructive, while others see it as the most empirically accurate way to communicate the urgency and severity of the crisis, potentially leading to overwhelm rather than action.
